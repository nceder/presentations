Part #1 - Motivations

  • This time last year
  • I was actually working at The Guardian when this story broke
  • The result was a...

Moral Panic

  • Snowden leaks confirmed or exceeded our worst suspicions
  • Government launched a response / defense
  • If you have nothing to hide you have nothing to fear.
  • A classic defence of the situation along with "think of the children", terrorists, extremists and (heaven forbid) hackers.
  • Not just a problem in the "public" government realm.
  • For years "private" corporations have insinuated themselves into our lives
  • This has been worrying *me* for a long time (since pre-2010)
  • Corporate speak re-packaging of the "nothing to fear" argument.
  • Another formulation, "only if you're doing something wrong should you worry, and then you don't deserve to keep it private"
  • We don't want the bad guys to gain the upper hand, you're probably a fine upstanding citizen and we should be happy that innocents are protected from the evil-doers that such a drag net will identify.

WRONG!

It's a false dichotemy.

  • It's not you who determines if you have anything to hide.
  • It assumes surveillance results in correct data and sound judgement.
  • Rules change.
  • Breaking the law isn't necessarily bad (viz. Ghandi, Jesus, Mandela, Socrates).
  • Those who say privacy is dead are the ones that gain the most from surveillance.
  • Privacy is a fundamental human right.

Politics

What has that got to do with programming?

  • This is all very political!
  • Hang on a minute? This is a technology conference.
  • What has politics to do with programming?

We're engineers!

We ask questions like...

  • What is the best way to organise computational resources? (Questions about architecture & design.)
  • How should such arrangements be created? (TDD, Agile, with Python etc...)
  • Who or what is responsible for making things work? (Standards, roles, authorisation.)

A straw-man engineer might say things like:

  • After all, we're engineers who love to solve engineering problems.
  • I don't worry about the "politics" of stuff, I'm far more interested in the hard technical problems.

Contrast these problems with political philosophy

Political Philosophers!

Asks questions like...

  • What is the best way to organise humanity? (Forms of government: democracy, corporate structures.)
  • How should such arrangements be created? (Duty, rights, the law etc...)
  • Who or what is responsible for making things work? (Power, authority, governance.)

    • This not politics nor politicians but political philosophy and philosophers. Nevertheless there is some overlap.

Programming is politics

we're asking and answering questions about organisation, process, power & control.

  • We are writing, implementing and, in some respects, control the laws of the digital world.
  • Are we blinded by the engineering problems into ignoring the political aspects of our work?

Is Peer-to-Peer the answer to concerns over power & control in a digital world?

  • Myself, Holger and many others believe that it probably is (or is an important aspect of the solution).
  • Let's examine what P2P means and how this affects the political aspects of this talk.

What is Peer-to-Peer (P2P)..?

Peers of equal status (devices running appropriate software) cooperate in a loose decentralised network for mutual benefit. Peer-to-peer is the antithesis of hierarchy - where some have elevated status and power over others.

Peer-to-Peer (left) vs. Client/Server (right)

  • That red spot is a point of power and control.
  • Power and control = politics
  • Consider how this affects the WWW
The client/server architecture of the web is a fundamentally unbalanced because the server always has power over the client.
The server is a single point of failure that is also the obvious target of attacks.

(Although sometimes hierarchy is good)

(Especially when command and control saves lives.)

In an ideal world, hierarchy is best when the obvious skill, knowledge and capabilities of an entity are acknowledged to bring about greater benefit for all.

In an ideal world, those with elevated status and authority would have earned it via reliable and consistent public displays of such skill, knowledge and capabilities.

In an ideal world, the responsibility and trust associated with status and authority would be a serious yet welcome obligation.

We do not live in an ideal world

:-(

  • We live in a digital world where architecture in some sense defines power and control.
  • So what can we do?

Part #4 - Outcomes

We've worked on several software ideas: p2p chat and DHT/drogulus (describe these). A fundamental problem is discoverablility - P4P2P is one way to aid this through DHT within DHT (or namespaced DHTs). Many solutions already exist but only a few have had any success (mainly Bittorrent, Bitcoin in new-ish tech - although many examples from old tech like email and IRC) What are the economic motivations for P2P. A worthy cause doesn't make a business case (at best it's merely a charity case). Call to arms. BoF on P2P this evening. If you're interested come along and let's organise.

P2P Chat

Holger's "test card". Several of the group already involved in chat based technology (Crypho).

Distributed Hash Tables

My current obsession as the drogulus.

  • A distributed and decentralised dictionary / data store;
  • No single point of failure or inadvertent point of control (e.g. no need for DNS);
  • Scales to huge numbers of nodes (redundancy++);
  • Relatively efficient;
  • Good handling of fluid network membership;
  • Solid foundation for infrastructure (resource discovery, key/value store, CDN etc...)
  • Tested in the real world (Bittorrent, Freenet & others).
